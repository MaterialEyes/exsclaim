Interaction of titanium dioxide nanoparticles with soil components and plants: current knowledge and future research needs – a critical review

Abstract

Titanium dioxide nanoparticles (nano-TiO2), one of the most produced engineered nanoparticles (ENPs), are used in pigments, photocatalysis, food additives, and personal care products. This broad variety of applications has led to the unusual and widespread distribution of nano-TiO2 in several environmental sectors, with different effects on living organisms. In the last decade, several publications have shown fragments of information about the interaction, detection, uptake, and translocation of nano-TiO2 in plants. This review includes a discussion of the current knowledge about factors affecting the interaction of nano-TiO2 with soil and plants. We also discuss the role of particle size, crystal phase, surface coating, and techniques employed to study the interaction of these nanoparticles with plants. Concluding results from synchrotron based, microscopic, physiological, and biochemical analyses of plants exposed to nano-TiO2 are presented. However, the current information leaves no doubt that there are still many aspects in need of additional investigations to fully understand the effects of nano-TiO2 in plants. For instance, little is known about the transgenerational effects of nano-TiO2 exposure and the changes at agronomical and physiological levels. The effects of such ENPs in proteins and other metabolites are also not well understood. In addition, more information is needed about the interaction of nano-TiO2 with other ENPs-and-organic co-contaminates and the effects of plants. Since nano-TiO2 have been found in edible tissues, it is expected that they will be in the food chain; thus, studies on their trophic transfer are required. The authors hope that, besides contributing to a better understanding of current data about the effects of nano-TiO2 in soil and plants, this review will help to drive future investigations and will contribute to the general knowledge on the environmental interactions of engineered nanomaterials.


Environmental significance

Titanium dioxide nanoparticles (nano-TiO2) are among the most consumed nanomaterials worldwide. Studies have shown important pieces of information regarding the response of living organisms to the interaction with nano-TiO2. State-of-the-art techniques have been used to track these nanoparticles through plant tissues and to measure the effects on plant metabolism. This review includes a comprehensive analysis of the response of plants, at different growth stages, exposed to nano-TiO2 at different concentrations, particle size, crystal phase, and surface chemistry. Effects of growth media, advantages of analytical techniques, concluding data, and future research needs are discussed. The authors intend to provide research questions to better understand the effects of nano-TiO2 in plants and, ultimately, the environmental implications of nanotechnology.

1. Introduction

Titanium dioxide nanoparticles (nano-TiO2) have become essential parts of our daily life. They are used in food additives, paints, glass, personal care, cosmetics, solar cells, and wastewater treatment.1 The production of nano-TiO2, for 2010, was estimated to be 88000 tons,1 and is expected to continuously increase in the near future.

TiO2 is found in three crystal phases. The rutile phase is the most stable, while anatase and brookite are transition forms of rutile.2 Due to its excellent electrical and optical properties, originated from the shorter Ti–O distances in their lower symmetry octahedron structure, the anatase form is widely utilized in sensors, batteries, solar cells,2 and photocatalysts.3 The rutile phase has the highest thermal stability, due to a slight orthorhombic distortion, and higher symmetry than anatase.2 It is widely used in personal care and cosmetic products.4 The brookite phase is common in natural and synthetic nanoscale samples. Different from the tetragonal structure of anatase and rutile, brookite is orthorhombic.2 It has a larger cell volume (eight TiO2 groups), which makes it less stable than the anatase (four TiO2 groups) and rutile (two TiO2 groups) phase.5 To the best of authors' knowledge, there are no commercial nano-TiO2 products in the brookite phase. A mixture of anatase and rutile (P25) is widely used as pigment in coatings, plastic, and glass.6 It is reported that a combination of anatase and rutile resulted in a higher solar-to-electric energy conversion efficiency than the pure phases of nano-TiO2.7

Since an abundant number of products contain nano-TiO2, it is expected that after the useful life of the products ends, a large amount of nano-TiO2 is released into the environment reaching air, soil, and water; eventually, interacting with living organisms. Efforts have been done to measure the environmental nano-TiO2 in soil and water. Using single particle inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (sp-ICP-MS), Gondikas et al.8 reported that the average concentration of TiO2 in the Old Danube lake water during a year period was less than 1.7 μg L−1. Using the same analytical technique, Reed et al.9 found that in the Clear Creek in Golden, Colorado, during the summer, concentrations of TiO2 were in the range of ng L−1, concentrations that have shown to be nontoxic to bacteria or Daphnia magna. Using new mathematical models and calculation methods, some research groups have estimated the production and emissions of nano-TiO2 and other engineered nanoparticles (ENPs).1,10–13 Keller and Lazareva1 estimated the nano-TiO2 concentrations in San Francisco Bay. They reported concentrations of 10–70 μg L−1 in wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) effluents and concentrations were as high as 1000 mg kg−1 in biosolids. Gottschalk et al.14 found that nano-TiO2 particles used in personal care and cosmetics have been found in sludge in the range of 69 to 1500 mg kg−1. In addition, by using state-of-the-art analytical instruments and biochemical assays, researchers have studied the fate, behavior, and metabolic effects of ENPs in plants. General information has been presented in previous literature reviews.15–21

In this review article, we have discussed available data concerning nano-TiO2 and their interaction with plants. Contradictory results have been reported about the effects of nanoparticulate forms on plants at different growth stages.22–24 Detection techniques and possible effects of particle size, crystal phase, and surface coating are discussed. An analysis of the future research needs is also included.

2. Nano-TiO2 in the environment

Fig. 1 depicts several aspects regarding the use and release pathways, estimated amounts in environmental sectors, and possible interactions of plants with nano-TiO2. Plants, the primary producers, are directly exposed to nanomaterials, including TiO2, through the soil, water, and air in different proportions. It is reported that the emission of nano-TiO2 represents more than one fourth of the estimated mass flow of engineered nanomaterials in a worldwide range.1 Among all the applications, personal care products, pigments, and food additives contribute the most to the total environmental release.1 In addition, recent estimates indicate that 13.8% of the released nano-TiO2 is discharged into soil, 18.5% into water, and 2.2% in the air.1 Although WWTPs can manage and remove most of the nano-TiO2, particles with sizes less than 30 nm can still escape and accumulate in agricultural soils.4 The estimated concentration of nano-TiO2 for personal care products is up to 280 mg kg−1 in sediments of fresh water and 130–3100 μg kg−1 in sludge treated soil.14 This suggests that a portion of these particles are exposed directly to plants, providing ample opportunity for interaction with them. Fig. 2 presents the direct interactions between plant systems and nano-TiO2. Several investigations have shown that nano-TiO2 can penetrate root cells (Fig. 2A),22 translocate from roots to stems25 (Fig. 2B) and leaves (Fig. 2C),23 and even fruit (Fig. 2D).26 By using micro-X-ray absorption near the edge structure (XANES), Servin et al.23 reported that the xylem of cucumber plants had the same forms of nano-TiO2 added to the soil (82% anatase and 18% rutile). However, the data suggest that most of the nano-TiO2 remain in the root22,24 and, moreover, there is a lack of information regarding the uptake and translocation mechanisms.21,22,27,28 Due to the photoactive nature, nano-TiO2, can increase the activity of reactive oxygen species under light exposure.29 Thus, different physiological and biochemical parameters have shown to be affected including photosynthesis (Fig. 2E),30 metabolism (Fig. 2F),31 and gene expression (Fig. 2G),32 among others.

3. Effects of soil components on the behavior of nano-TiO2

Soil is the most essential and complicated matrix for plant growth. Plants interact with microbial communities, natural organic matter (NOM), and soil minerals. Besides, soil pH plays an important role in affecting the fate of nano-TiO2. Thus, after ENPs enter into soil, these factors will affect their behavior, mobility, and bioavailability.33 Unlike many other types of ENPs, nano-TiO2 are very stable. Schmidt and Vogelsberger34 reported a dissolution in water of 21.6 ± 2.7 μmol L−1 after 907.3 h at a temperature of 37 °C and pH of 1.5. In addition, the behavior of nano-TiO2 depends on the soil type (clay content and composition).35 The interaction between soil and nano-TiO2 is displayed in Fig. 3(A and B) and 4. Fig. 3(A) presents the factors of soil affecting behaviors of nano-TiO2. The characteristics of soil, such as pH, NOM, ionic strength, and clay contents would influence the transport of nano-TiO2 by affecting their zeta potential, surface charge/affinity, aggregation, and van der Waals force.36,37 Notably, for these soil factors, instead of affecting the behavior of nano-TiO2 individually, they are usually associated with each other.

3.1 Natural organic matter and pH

NOM consists of humic and non-humic fractions (such as fulvic acid and citric acid). Each fraction has different water solubility and reactivity.33,38 NOM can affect the transport of nano-TiO2 by altering the surface properties of soil particles.39 The possible interactions between nano-TiO2 and NOM include adsorption onto uncoated NPs, over coating or displacement of the original coating of nano-TiO2, and bridging NPs.31Fig. 4 displays the mechanisms by which NOM interacts with nano-TiO2 with different crystal phases and surface properties. Regardless of size, concentration, synthesis method, or point of zero charges, anatase and rutile nano-TiO2, with no surface coating, present a negative charge (Fig. 4D and E).24,27,40
Fig. 4 Interaction of NOM with nano-TiO2 of different crystal phase and surface properties: (A) soil colloids coated with negatively charged and hydrophilic (red circle) natural organic matters, (B) positively charged nano-TiO2 with a hydrophilic coating, (C) negatively charged nano-TiO2 with a hydrophilic coating, (D) negatively charged plain anatase (yellow dot) nano-TiO2, (E) negatively charged plain rutile (pink dot) nano-TiO2, (F) negatively charged nano-TiO2 with a hydrophobic (blue circle) coating, (G) positively charged nano-TiO2 with a hydrophobic coating.

The absolute value of the zeta potential reflects the stability of particle dispersions. Nanoparticles with zeta potentials between 0 and 5 (positive or negative) are unstable and tend to aggregate rapidly, whereas the stability of ENPs increases when zeta potential increases (±10 to ±30, incipient instability; ±30 to ±40, moderate stability; ±40 to ±60, good stability; more than ±61 mV, excellent stability).36 The charge of zeta potential also depends on the surface coatings of particles. It is known that functional groups like –COOH or –OH, result in negative charge, and functional groups such as –NH2, –N–(CH3)2, or –Si–(CH3)3 yields positive charges.24,41,42 Due to the dissociation of H+ ions from the groups of carboxylic (–COOH) and phenolic (–OH), humus colloidal surfaces that are negatively charged and have hydrophilic coatings (Fig. 4A), will attract positively charged particles (Fig. 4B), as well as hydrophilic nano-TiO2 (with a function group of –COOH, –OH, –NH2, or –SO3−) (Fig. 4B and C) via electrostatic and hydrogen bonding.33,43 These attractions usually result in decreases of zeta potential (from positive to negative) of nano-TiO2, which will result in better mobility and increased stability in soil.43 In particles with negative charge (Fig. 4D–F) or hydrophobic coatings (with a functional group of R–Si(CH3)3, R–CHO, or R1–O–R2) (Fig. 4F and G), the NOM would possibly absorb, overcoat or displace the original nano-TiO2, changing the surface properties (charge and affinity), enhancing the steric repulsion, and reducing deposition and aggregation of ENPs.

ENPs with higher negative charges are believed to exhibit more uniform distribution and mobility in soil matrix due to the enhanced steric repulsion between them and soil colloids, or between ENPs themselves.36 It has been reported that the presence of NOM, such as humic acid, can stabilize the zeta potential of nano-TiO2 as negative, regardless of the ionic strength, and alter the surface properties of ENPs from hydrophobicity to hydrophilicity (Fig. 4D–G), which reduces the opportunities for deposition and aggregation.36,44 Moreover, the aggregation of nano-TiO2 decreases with the increasing coating of NOM, while the surface charge increases.36 This was confirmed by a study about the coating of anatase nano-TiO2 with citric acid at different pH values.45

It is well-known that the optimal pH range for the growth of most plants is between 5.5 and 7.0. Thus, it is noteworthy to determine the relationship of pH and NOM to the behaviors of ENPs. Studies indicate that the adsorption of NOM on nano-TiO2 was pH-dependent, and the adsorpted amount was limited by ENPs' surface area.43 Mudunkotuwa et al.45 found that the adsorption of citric acid on nano-TiO2, and the aggregation degree of particles, changed as a function of pH. At pH 6.0, nano-TiO2 showed large aggregates and instability, while the suspension was stable and aggregation was impeded in the presence of citric acid. At pH 2.0, nano-TiO2 suspensions are stable, with limited bioavailability in the presence of citric acid. Overall, the interaction of NOM on the bioavailability of nano-TiO2 is associated with the concentration of NOM, pH, and molecular structure.46

3.2 Ionic strength

The ionic strength (concentration of ions in solution) is another important factor influencing the migration of ENPs in soil.28 The ionic strength depends on the pH value, ionic charge, and electrolyte type.44 When there is an increased salt concentration in soil, the ionic strength would increase. Consequently, the attractive force between particles in soil will surpass the steric repulsion and the aggregation of particles will increase, leading to a lower ENPs' mobility.47 The effects of ionic strength are usually studied in combination with changes in pH and NOM; the results have shown minimal impacts of such a combination on the behaviors of nano-TiO2.39 However, the contribution of ionic strength should not be neglected.

3.3 Clay

The properties of soil depend on the composition and content of clays. The negative charged layer silicates are the most abundant inorganic colloids in soils.48 Kaolinite [Al2O3·2SiO2·2H2O, 1:1-type silicate clay (one tetrahedral silica and one octahedral alumina)] and montmorillonite [(Na,Ca)0.33(Al,Mg)2(Si4O10)(OH)2·nH2O, 2:1-type silicate clay (two tetrahedral silica and one octahedral alumina)] are the dominant layer silicate clays in soils.48 Bentonite particles (Al2O3·4SiO2·2H2O) are majorly consisting of montmorillonite, which is thinner, but with higher plasticity, greater dry strength and shrinkage than kaolinite.49,50 It is reported that the transport of nano-TiO2 was significantly retained in the soils with higher clay contents.35 Cai et al.50 studied the effects of two representative clay particles, kaolinite and bentonite, on the mobility of nano-TiO2 in quartz sand. They found that effects depended on the electrolyte type. In NaCl solution, the mobility of nano-TiO2 was increased by both types of clay particles. Whereas, in CaCl2 solution, kaolinite decreased the ENPs transport, and bentonite increased their mobility. Clusters with greater size are likely contributing, significantly, in the different mobility of particles. In NaCl, smaller nano-TiO2-kaoline clusters were observed; while larger clusters were observed in CaCl2, nano-TiO2-kaoline.50 However, there are no studies regarding the effects of montmorillonite on the mobility of nano-TiO2.

3.4 Soil microorganism

The soil properties affected by the accumulation of ENPs are displayed in Fig. 3(B). Microbial communities are among the most abundant and diverse organisms on earth. They are critical to soil quality because they decompose organic material and recycle minerals.49 The existence of ENPs might directly/indirectly alter the population, diversity, and activity of bacteria by changing soil water, total suspended solids (TSS), total oxygen demand (TOD), electrical conductivity (EC), soil enzymes, and nutritional elements.22,50–53 The interaction of ENPs and microbial communities are varied in different soil types.53,54 There are controversial results about the effects of nano-TiO2 in soil enzymes.22,50,53,55 At low exposure concentration (91 mg L−1), Du et al.22 found nano-TiO2 significantly increased urease activity. However, at high exposure concentrations (1000 mg L−1), Chai et al.57 and You et al.55 reported that nano-TiO2 resulted in significantly lower urease activity than controls, despite the soil type and exposure times. Since there is a positive correlation between urease activity and nitrogen fixation56 the results suggest that 1000 mg L−1 of nano-TiO2 affect microorganisms involved in nitrogen fixation.53 In other reports, the soil exposed to nano-TiO2 showed significantly lower catalase, invertase, phosphatase, and peroxidase activities.22,53 Nano-TiO2 also caused no effect or significantly higher soil protease activity.22,50 Intensive studies indicated that nano-TiO2 reduced microbial biomass and diversity and altered the composition of microbial communities in a dose-dependent manner.58 Moreover, it is reported that the gene expression of bacteria was disrupted, resulting in declining presence of taxa related to nitrogen fixation, methane oxidation, and increases in populations from taxa associated with the decomposition of organic pollutants and proteins,50 and down-regulated stress-related gene profile.59

4. Techniques and platforms employed to study the interaction of nano-TiO2 with plants

Detection methods and techniques used to study interactions between nano-TiO2 and plant systems are summarized in Fig. 5. Most of the studies have been focused on tracking the fate, translocation and biotransformation of ENPs in plants. Electron microscopy (scanning electron microscopy and transmission electron microscopy) and inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectroscopy/mass spectroscopy (ICP-OES/MS) are among the most frequently used detection instruments (Fig. 5).60,61 However, the tedious sample preparation and low resolution of electron microscopy make it less convenient and more difficult for ENPs localization inside plant cells.62 Moreover, the digestion of a sample containing nano-TiO2 for ICP-OES/MS analysis brings potential risks and requires skilled personnel. The hydrofluoric acid,63 mixtures of acids (sulfuric acid, nitric acid and hydrogen peroxide),24,64 or perchloric acid65 usually used to digest samples could represent health hazards. In addition, high temperatures (170– 240 °C)66 and pressures (11 ± 3 bar)64 are necessary in traditional detection workflows, which can bring underlying risks. By using an enzymatic extraction, which overcomes difficulties from traditional digestion methods, Dan et al.67 analyzed the uptake of nano-Au in tomatoes with single-particle inductively coupled plasma-mass spectrometry (spICP-MS). After that, Deng et al.68 examined its applicability for nano-TiO2 determination and compared the results with those collected from traditional acid digestion by spICP-MS. They found higher number of particles and particles of wider size from acid digestion, which indicates there is a need to find more efficient digestion procedures. The spICP-MS not only helps in the particle quantification, the technique also allows to measure the size and distribution of NPs (spICP-MS can discriminate particles with a size of 10 nm). However, it requires multiple analyses and statistical work.60,69 Furthermore, unlike metal-based ENPs including nano-CuO, nano-ZnO, nano-CeO2, or nano-Ag, nano-TiO2 has very high stability and low dissolution rate.34 In addition, the root-to shoot uptake is reported to be low, compared to other ENPs. Larue et al.25 reported a concentration of 99 ± 89 mg kg−1 of Ti in the vascular system of hydroponically cultivated lettuce exposed to 1000 mg L−1 of nano-TiO2. Moreover, no study has reported the biotransformation of nano-TiO2 after uptake by plants.20 To conquer this challenge, techniques with higher sensitivity and lower damage, like two-photon electron microscopy (TPEM)70 and synchrotron radiation,23 could be widely applied in future studies (Fig. 5). In a report of using TPEM to image translocation of ENPs in plant systems, Wild et al.70 found nano-TiO2 aggregates on the wheat root surface, but not inside root cells. TPEM has outstanding advantages such as no need of staining and capability to be applied in the living specimens. However, it is difficult to identify the composition of nanoparticles without coupling other techniques, such as energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS).70 By using μ-X-ray fluorescence (μ-XRF) and μ-X-ray absorption near edge structure (μ-XANES), it has been shown that nano-TiO2 translocate from roots to stems,25 leaf trichomes,23 and even fruit.26 Synchrotron-based techniques can achieve higher resolutions than electrical and optical microscopy. However, there are limitations in the available time at the facilities and such techniques requires high concentrations of the targeted ENPs in the samples.71 For instance, Castillo-Michel et al.71 mentioned that X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) requires a factor of 10 higher concentration in tissues than the mass of particles. Recently, by synthesizing nano-TiO2 labeled with the stable isotope 47Ti, Bourgeault et al.72 broadened our sight to track the bioaccumulation of nano-TiO2, which could be an alternative approach. The technique allowed to determine the compartmentalization of Ti supplied to the target organisms through ICP-MS, which suggest these particle can be tracked in tissues using such analytical technique.72

Effects of nano-TiO2 in antioxidant responses (such as enzymatic activities, H2O2 production, and lipid peroxidation) have also been widely examined by employing UV/vis and fluorescence microscopy.75 In addition, nutritional compounds (carbohydrates, proteins, fatty acids, and amino acids) or micro/macro-elements have been studied by Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR),76 UV/vis, gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC-MS), and ICP-OES77 (Fig. 5). However, the mechanisms involved in the distortion of physiological and biochemical processes by nano-TiO2 are still largely unknown. Recently, proteomic and metabolic studies of other ENPs in plants systems (e.g. nano-CeO2 and nano-Cu) have touched the baseline of interaction mechanisms between ENPs and plants. For example, using GC-MS, Majumdar et al.78 found that stress-related proteins, nutrient storage compounds, and carbohydrates metabolism were influenced in seeds exposed to nano-CeO2. Zhao et al.79–81 employed proton nuclear magnetic resonance (1H NMR), gas chromatography-time-of-flight mass spectrometry (GC-ToF-MS), and ICP-MS to examine metabolomics and metallomics mechanisms in lettuce and cucumber plants treated with copper-based nanoparticles. There is no doubt that these analytical approaches will be extended to study more in depth the interactions between nano-TiO2 and plant systems.

5. Factors affecting the interaction of nano-TiO2 with plants

5.1 Particle size

Tables 1–3 summarize the size, crystal phase of nano-TiO2, exposure mode, and detection techniques applied to study their interaction with plant systems. Plant cells, unlike animal cells, are surrounded by the cell wall. This special and extra barrier has pores with diameters ranging from 5–30 nm.82–84 Nano-TiO2 with a size less than 30 nm are very likely to enter plants through these cell wall pores. In addition, it is well-known that the band gap energy of nano-TiO2 increases as their size decreases.85 Thus, the size of nano-TiO2 could be one of the essential factors affecting the photosynthetic system and other biochemical processes in plants. It is reported that the mixture of anatase and rutile nano-TiO2 reduced root pore size, water flow, and root hydraulic potential in corn.86 Larue et al.27 examined the uptake of nano-TiO2 with different particle sizes in wheat (14, 22, 25, 36, 140, and 655 nm) and found that 140 nm was the threshold diameter beyond which accumulation in plant roots was no longer observed. These researchers also reported that smaller nano-TiO2 (12, 22, and 25 nm) translocated from roots to leaves, while particles with a size of 36 nm accumulated in parenchyma but did not reach the stele. Similarly, by using electron microscopy, it was reported that particles larger than 30 nm, also penetrated the root cell,87 indicating that nano-TiO2 possibly enlarged cell pores or created new ones. More studies and explanations, with respect to the pore size change by nanoparticles, are urgently needed.

5.2 Crystal phase

It is believed that properties of ENPs varied with the crystal phases.2 Thus, there is no doubt that the different crystal phases of nano-TiO2 would result in dissimilar interactions with plants. As can be seen in Tables 1–3, anatase nano-TiO2 interrupted antioxidant stress in duckweed40 and tomato,91 decreased photosynthetic rate in long raceme elm,30 induced chromosomal aberrations in onion,32 affected arbuscular mycorrhizal fungal communities in soybean,92 and delayed and reduced germination rates in rice.93 However, it increased elongation and biomass in spinach88 and parsley.94 Similar to anatase, rutile phase affected photosynthetic parameters, such as the oxygen release rate from chloroplasts95 and chlorophyll formation96 in spinach. Other studies have shown differences in the uptake and translocation between nano-TiO2 phases. Servin et al.23 reported that rutile phase showed preferential uptake in cucumber tissues exposed to a mixture of anatase/rutile; while another study showed that anatase translocated into rice root but the rutile phase did not.28 Spengler et al.97 reported that, independently of the crystal phase, all the nano-TiO2 treatments elevated enzyme activity in Hydrilla verticillata. In general, plants have shown varied responses to nano-TiO2 with different crystal phases. It might be due to differences in particle properties, such as zeta potential,24,35 cell volume,5 stability, and band gaps (rutile phase, 3.0 eV vs. anatase phase, 3.2 eV).1 According to the literature, anatase displayed higher toxicity than rutile phase, which could be attributed to the larger aggregates formed by rutile that reduces the possibility to penetrate into plant cells.98 To the best of the authors' knowledge, no studies with respect to brookite have been published yet. It could be possibly due to brookite's limited applications. Further studies are urgently needed to investigate impacts and mechanism of different crystal phases of nano-TiO2 on plant systems.

5.3 Surface coating

ENPs are encapsulated with inorganic or organic compounds to avoid aggregation and achieve better dispersion, as well as to enhance the optical performance.2 Then, the surface properties of nano-TiO2 (surface area, zeta potential, and surface affinity) would be substantially altered.24 Thus, it is assumed that the interaction between coated ENPs and plants would be different from the pristine particles. Yang et al.99 reported that humic acid coated nano-TiO2 significantly enhanced phenathrene sorption, which indicates higher toxicity to the environment. Kurepa et al.100 reported that in Arabidopsis thaliana, alizarin red S stained nano-TiO2 accumulated in nuclei and vacuoles of root and leaf stomata without showing adverse effects on cell viability and antioxidant stress; however, it produced cell morphology changes.73 Nano-TiO2 with a hydrophobic coating (coated with aluminum hydroxide and dimethicone films) internalized in faba bean root and caused antioxidant stress.101 In a short-term foliar study, Larue et al.64 exposed lettuce to a weathered paint leachate containing nano-TiO2 (82% anatase, 18% rutile), and seven days after exposure, they identified Ti in all plant tissues. Anatase nano-TiO2 with an activated carbon layer was found to result in enhanced seed germination of tomato and mungbean.102 Very recently, Tan et al.24 reported different physiological effects and Ti uptake in basil exposed to pristine, hydrophobic (coated with aluminum oxide and dimethicone), and hydrophilic (coated with aluminum oxide and glycerol) nano-TiO2. Basil plants preferentially accumulated Ti from hydrophobic particles in the root.24

It is worthy to mention that doping a small amount of a metal or metalloid improves the optical property of nano-TiO2.2 Additionally, surfactants such as cetyltrimethylammonium bromide, are used to stabilize nano-TiO2.103 However, the roles of coating materials, doping chemicals, and surfactants should be studied in the near future, if we want to have a holistic picture of the effects of ENPs in the environment.

6.1 Petri dish studies

Table 1 shows a summary of results from Petri dish studies. Seeds have been directly cultured in nano-TiO2 suspension,104 or firstly germinated in individual Petri dishes and then exposed to nano-TiO2 as embryos105 or seedlings100 for several hours or days. Anatase and a mixture of anatase/rutile (82%/18%) (with size ranging from 2.8 nm to 100 nm) have been mostly tested (Table 1). TEM and μ-X-ray fluorescence have shown nano-TiO2 in nuclei and vacuoles of Arabidopsis thaliana root (root exposure) and leaf stomata (foliar exposure),100 in faba bean root peripheral,101 in parenchymal and vascular cylinder of wheat root,106 and in barley root cortical parenchyma and xylem.107

Most of the reports from Petri dish studies focused on genotoxicity assessments including alterations in cell or DNA structure damages,73 mitotic index,32 cell viability,100 chromosomal aberrations,104 or induced DNA/mRNA expression,108 among others. By using florescence microscopy, confocal microscopy, and other biochemical assays, concentration-dependent increased chromosomal aberrations were found in onion roots exposed to 12.5–100 μg L−1 nano-TiO2. At the lowest concentration (12.5 μg L−1), authors observed chromosomal aberrations; however, the mitotic index was altered only at 50 and 100 μg L−1.32 Authors observed significant increases in ROS, which they considered as the cause of the DNA aberrations. Ghosh et al.104 reported an increase in malondialdehyde content in onion at 4 mM treatment dose of nano-TiO2 suggesting that the lipid peroxidation caused the DNA damage. Furthermore, epidermal and stomatal guard cells disruption and tubulin aggregation have been observed in Arabidopsis thaliana.73 Wang et al.73 concluded that this occurred either by a physical effects of the binding of nano-TiO2 with microtubules or by an increase in ROS. These results point out that an increase in ROS caused by nano-TiO2 plays a significant role in the DNA damage. These results can explain the physiological and biochemical impacts of nano-TiO2 in enzyme activities,40 biomass production, plant elongation,98 and seed germination.110 In addition, germination time or germination rate have been increased in parsley,94 flax,98 tomato, mungbean,102 tobacco,108 cabbage,110 and fennel.111 Frazier et al.108 indicate that nano-TiO2 activate the expression profiles of microRNAs (miRNAs), gene regulators that not only participate in plant development but also increase plant tolerance to abiotic stresses. Contrasting results have been reported from corn and wheat,89 rice,90 narbon vetch,109 soybean,110 and cucumber.112 However, no effects were observed in tomato,91 carrot, lettuce, oat, onion, and ryegrass.110 Although the specific action mechanisms have not been described, Andersen et al.110 suggest that the photocatalytic activity of nano-TiO2 could have an effect at seed coat level. It is also possible that differences in the genetic constitution of the varieties, perhaps at miRNAs level, contributes to the different effects. Nano-TiO2 have shown to affect antioxidant activity include superoxide dismutase (SOD), peroxidase (POD), catalase (CAT) in duckweed,40 ascorbate peroxidase (APOX) in faba bean,101 malondialdehyde (MDA) in onion and tobacco,104 and thiols (GSH) in narbon bean.113 Song et al.40 mentioned that in duckweed, nano-TiO2 increased the enzyme activity when the exposure concentration was lower than 200 mg L−1, while 500 mg L−1 caused serious damage to plant cells. Although with variations in doses, similar results have been reported for the above mentioned plants. This suggests that the effects on enzyme activity is variety- and -concertation-dependent.

6.2 Short-term studies

A summary of short-term studies with nano-TiO2 in plant system is displayed in Table 2. Anatase, rutile, and mixture of anatase/rutile (82%/18%) nano-TiO2 (with a size ranging from 4 to 655 nm) have been examined. The nano-TiO2 exposure duration of each study varies between the plant species (7–60 days), but they were conducted before fruits or grain maturity. Beside the effects of nano-TiO2, other co-influential factors, such as mild heat stress,114 soil type,115 and water-deficit116 were also studied. Exposure has been made through the roots or the leaves. Twenty four studies included root exposure and seven foliar exposure; while 12 studies were performed in hydroponics (Table 2).
6.2.1 Root exposure. Relatively, few papers have described the uptake and translocation of nano-TiO2 in plants, perhaps due to difficulties with the digestion process.22,24,64,117 Furthermore, to the best of the authors' knowledge, no studies have reported the biotransformation of nano-TiO2 in plant tissues. One of the problems about studies with TiO2 in plants has been the high concentrations used. Gottschalk et al.11 reported that the estimated concentrations of nano-TiO2 in sludge are between 107–802 mg kg−1. In some cases, the amended concentration has been several times higher than the predicted environmental concentrations. For instances, Jośko et al.115 used 10000 mg L−1 in a study with garden cress (Lepidium sativum) grown in soil. In a hydroponic study, cucumber was exposed to 4000 mg L−1 of nano-TiO2 and authors detected Ti in leaf trichomes.23 In another hydroponic study with wheat, Ti was observed in roots treated with 100 mg L−1 anatase or rutile with different sizes (14–655 nm), while only NPs with sizes under 30 nm translocated to leaves.27 In addition, another study showed higher Ti content in rapeseed than wheat tissues, indicating that Ti accumulation is species-dependent.106 Recently, Liu et al.118 found that rutile reduced the toxicity of tetracycline, and conversely, the presence of tetracycline significantly decreased Ti accumulation in both shoots and roots.

Although the uptake or translocation of nano-TiO2 is lower than other nanoparticles, nano-TiO2 (rutile, anatase, or mixture of anatase and rutile) have been reported to increase the release rate of oxygen in chloroplasts,119 photosynthetic rate96 and chlorophyll formation/content88 in spinach, and photosystem energy dissipation in tomato.114 In contrast, other reports showed no effects on chlorophyll content in common duckweed20 or reductions in CO2 assimilation efficiency in elegant clarkia (Clarkia unguiculata).117

6.2.2 Shoot exposure. Very few reports describe the effects of foliar exposure to nano-TiO2. Wang et al.121 applied 100 mg L−1 of nano-TiO2 on watermelon leaves for 15 days. The recovery rate of nano-TiO2 translocated into the leaf, stem, and root was 61.25%, 33.30%, and 5.45%, respectively. They assumed that the uptake of nano-TiO2 was through stomata. Later on, Larue et al.64 studied the fate of nano-TiO2 in lettuce and found the NPs distributed from leaves into all plant tissues. Subsequently, Raliya et al.122 hypothesized that nano-TiO2 directly penetrated through stomata, trichomes, hydathodes, lenticels, or cuticle wounds of tomato leaves; then, translocated to other plant tissues through phloem, a tissue in the vascular system. The foliar application of nano-TiO2 increased pigment (chlorophyll, carotenoid and anthocyanin) content, as well as photosynthesis in maize at the reproductive stage (appearance of male or female flower);90 however, it decreased net photosynthetic rate in long raceme elm,30 and had no effect in lettuce.64 This suggests that more studies, using different plants species, are needed in order to clarify the response of plants to the foliar application of nano-TiO2.

7. Conclusions and future research needs

The literature shows that more studies with ENMs are covering the full-life cycle of plants, since short term studies overestimate the ability of plants to recover from toxicants.15,123 However, very few studies have been performed with nano-TiO2.124 Although full life cycle studies of nano-TiO2 are less, compared to short term studies, they mimic real conditions and give a better estimate of the real effects of ENMs in plants.11 A summary of full-life cycle studies with nano-TiO2 in plant systems is displayed in Table 3. As seen in this table, very interesting results have been observed in fully developed plants. Servin et al.26 reported, for the first time, that cucumber plants translocated nano-TiO2 from the soil to the leaves and fruits. These researchers also reported increases of fruit phosphorus and potassium in nano-TiO2 exposed plants. In the case of barley, significant reductions in number of kernels and grain yield were observed in plant exposed to nano-TiO2 at 500 mg L−1.125 Du et al.126 evaluated the role of carbon dioxide on nano-TiO2 effects on rice. These researchers reported lower grain yield and biomass in soil amended with 200 mg L−1 of nano-TiO2, under high CO2 dose (570 μmol mol−1). Other researchers have reported alterations in nutritional elements in lettuce roots and shoots,127 stem and leaves of tomato,128 and soybean roots.129 In pinto bean plants sprayed with nano-TiO2 at different stages (rapid growth, flowering, and pod mature stages), there were changes in the antioxidant system.130 Although the current data do not show a clear trend in the effects of nano-TiO2 in plants, the facts that nano-TiO2 have been found in edible tissues, and that these ENMs are very stable, suggest that human beings could ingest them through produce harvested in nano-TiO2 impacted soils.

7.1 Effects of ENM mixture

In a realistic scenario, plants grow under exposure to mixtures of nanoparticles, rather than a single type of nanoparticle.11,131 So far, few studies with mixtures of nano-TiO2/nano-Ni/nano-ZnO,132 nano-TiO2/nano-ZnO,133 nano-TiO2/nano-Ag/nano-ZnO,134 and nano-TiO2/nano-CuO135 showed varied effects on plants. Some reports have revealed that the combination of nanoparticles can alleviate the symptoms caused by single nanoparticles.135 This has been explained by a decrease in dissolution rate of nanoparticles (such as nano-ZnO).133 The mixture of NPs results in a combination of interactions of ion-ion, ion-nanoparticles, and nanoparticles per se, regardless the interactions with soil clays, natural organic matter, and heavy metal.135 Thus, further and more specific studies should be implemented to figure out the complicated co-interaction of NP mixtures with plants.

7.2 Studies on the trophic transfer of nano-TiO2

Studies on the trophic transfer of ENMs are increasing.15 Data include studies with nano-CuO,136 nano-ZnO,137 nano-CeO2,138,139 single-walled140 and multi-walled carbon nanotubes.141 However, information on nano-TiO2 is limited.142 In addition, the foliar applications of nano-TiO2 as bactericide143 and fungicide,144 could represent a possible way for the transfer of such ENM within the food chain. Since nano-TiO2 can become entrapped in leaf stomata,64 insects like beetles, worms, birds, and herbivores, can move nano-TiO2 in the food web.15 Kubo-Irie et al.145 submerged wild nettle with oviposited butterfly eggs under 10 mg L−1 of P25 nano-TiO2 and found nano-TiO2 present not only in root hair, vascular tissue, and leaf veins, but also in the butterfly larvae. These results showed that nano-TiO2 were translocated from nettle to larvae and could distribute throughout the environment via larval excreta. Recently, researchers reported that after treating rats with food grade nano-TiO2 (E171), at a human relevant amount (200 μg or 10 mg kg−1 of body weight per day, oral exposure), it was observed that the systemic immune homeostasis was impaired and aberrant crypt foci were induced.146 Thus, it is urgent to investigate the trophic transfer of nano-TiO2 products in our terrestrial food chain.

7.3 Studies of transgenerational effects of nano-TiO2

A few reports have shown multigenerational effects of ENMs including nano-CeO2147–149 and nano-CuO.150 Lower biomass and water transpiration were found in the second generation of tomato seedlings sequentially exposed to 10 mg L−1 nano-CeO2.147 Lower seed production and higher oxidative stress were measured in the third generation of Brassica rapa, sequentially treated with 1000 mg L−1 nano-CeO2.148 In addition, alterations in gene expression and higher Cu accumulation were observed in Arabidopsis thaliana exposed to nano-CuO for two generations.150 In a recent study, Rico et al.149 found lower Ce accumulation and better growth performance in the second generation of wheat plants only treated in the first generation with nano-CeO2. To the best of authors' knowledge, no such studies regarding nano-TiO2 has been published yet. However, the fact that nano-TiO2 has been detected in fruits26 suggests that these ENMs can also be present in subsequent generations of plants.

7.4 Studies with commercial nano-TiO2 particles

To fulfill the demands of various applications (industry and daily life), nano-TiO2 particles with different shape, size, crystal phase, surface properties, and chemical composition have been synthesized, characterized, and commercially produced. Table 4 summarized the characteristics and applications of the most used commercially available nano-TiO2 particles. Among such, the fate and behavior of a mixture of anatase and rutile (P25) is the most widely studied (Tables 1–3). Very few studies have been focused on the phytotoxicity of food grade additive (E171)4 personal care products (M212 and M262),24 and photocatalyst (PC500 and UV100) (Table 4).151,152 Tan et al.24 were the first to report the effects of coated nano-TiO2, in rutile phase, on basil plants. Results showed that the hydrophobic surface given by dimethicone has the preferential accumulation in basil. Movafeghi et al.151 found that PC500 (anatase) significantly enhanced the superoxide dismutase activity but decreased plant growth and peroxidase activity in duckweed (S. polyrrhiza). Further and long-term studies are urgently needed to evaluate the fate, physiological, and biochemical impacts of the commercially available nano-TiO2 and other metal oxide nanoparticles in plants system.

7.5 Studies of molecular responses to nano-TiO2 exposure

Researchers have investigated gene expressions induced by nano-TiO2 exposure in plant systems.108,152–155 This information can provide a better understating of mechanism with respect to the reduced cell viability,100 alterations in organ morphology104 and mitotic index,109 as well as varied responses in plant growth, photosynthesis, metabolism, and transgenerational effects.149 Other molecular analyses, such as proteomics and metabolomics responses to nano-TiO2 exposure are still in its infancy. Recently, Wu et al.31 examined the effects on metabolomics on rice treated with nano-TiO2 for 14 days. Compared to controls, they found that 105 metabolites showed significant differences, basic energy-related pathways (pentose phosphate and tricarboxylic acid pathways) were enhanced, while carbohydrate synthesis metabolism was suppressed. By using GC-MS, Zahra et al.156 found that amino acid and glycerol contents were significantly increased, but fatty acids (linoleic, stearic, and oleic acids) were significantly reduced in rice cultivated in soil deficient in phosphorus. However, Zahra et al.156 analyzed samples exposed to 750 mg kg−1 nano-TiO2, data from lower exposure concentrations, more likely to be found in agricultural soils, are still missing. In addition, proteomic studies in plants exposed to nano-TiO2 have not been reported.

Perti dish studies have shown that nano-TiO2 induced gene expressions, which are associate with responding oxidative stress, water transport, salt stress, heavy metal uptake, and defending pathogen in tobacco108 and Arabidopsis thaliana.153 Short exposure studies has shown that nano-TiO2 treated plants resulted in 80 up-regulated genes and 74 down-regulated genes in Arabidopsis thaliana, whereas nano-ZnO and fullerene affected more genes (660 up-/826 down-regulation and 232 up-/189 down-regulation, respectively).152 Later on, by using the same plant model, Tumburu et al.155 reported that nano-TiO2 induced more alterations in gene expression than nano-CeO2, and the induced genes are related to photosynthesis. García-Sánchez et al.154 found that plants treated with nano-TiO2 resulted in decreased root hair production, which was due to the repressed RHS gene (in charge of root development). However, to the best of author's knowledge, only few papers have reported the transcriptomic responses of nano-TiO2. These studies are limited to Arabidopsis thaliana152–155 and tobacco,108 and no publications refer to full-life cycle or transgenerational stage. The transcriptional responses of CuO, CeO2, La2O3, and Cu(OH)2 have been studied in tomato, zucchi,157 and cucumber.29 A broadened scope, such as to study their long-term transcriptomic responses in more crop plants, are urgently needed.
