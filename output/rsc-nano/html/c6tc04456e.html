Are phosphide nano-cages better than nitride nano-cages? A kinetic, thermodynamic and non-linear optical properties study of alkali metal encapsulated X12Y12 nano-cages†

Khurshid Ayub
Department of Chemistry, COMSATS Institute of Information Technology, Abbottabad, 22060, Pakistan. E-mail: khurshid@ciit.net.pk

Received 14th October 2016 , Accepted 25th October 2016

First published on 10th November 2016

Abstract

Density functional theory calculations have been performed for alkali metal encapsulated X12Y12 nano-cages (X = B, Al and Y = N, P) to evaluate their stability, boundary crossing barriers and optical (linear and non-linear) properties. The adsorption energies of alkali metals in nano-cages are calculated, and correlated with the size of the nano-cages and alkali atoms. Distortion (expansion) of the nano-cages caused by alkali metal encapsulation was estimated through distortion energy. The distortion energies show good correlation with the diameter of the nano-cages. Kinetic barriers for the movement of alkali metals through nano-cages (boundary crossing barriers) are quantitatively measured. This manuscript presents the first ever study on boundary crossing barriers for alkali metal atoms through any spherical surface. The translation of alkali metals through the boundary of nano-cages presents a new approach for encapsulation of alkali metal atoms ((particularly lithium)) in nano-cages. The linear and non-linear optical properties of alkali metal encapsulated nano-cages are calculated. A quite remarkable non-linear optical response is calculated for lithium and potassium encapsulated boron phosphide (B12P12 or BP) nano-cages. In general, the non-linear optical response of phosphide nano-cages is two to three orders of magnitude higher than those of the corresponding nitride nano-cages. The calculated first hyperpolarizability of the K@BP nano-cage is 5.7 × 105 a.u., a value comparable to that of the best NLO material reported in the literature. The electronic structures of nano-cages including the HOMO–LUMO gap, TDOS, PDOS and excitation energies are analyzed to rationalize the extraordinary NLO response of the phosphide nano-cages. The NLO response of the K@BP nano-cage is primarily attributed to a very low excitation energy (0.5 eV). Interaction of nitrogen and phosphorus (of the nano-cages) with alkali metals differs between aluminum and boron nano-cages. Interaction of lone pair containing atoms with alkali metals in aluminum nano-cages generates diffuse excess electrons, whereas no such diffuse excess electrons are generated in boron nano-cages. UV-Vis and infra-red spectral characteristics for these encapsulated nano-cages are presented as a reference for future studies.

Introduction

Endohedral fullerenes were first anticipated in 19851 however, rapid exploration of their unique properties was made possible after the macroscopic production of fullerene in 1990.2 Endohedral metallofullerenes (EMFs) are a subclass of endohedral fullerenes which has extensively been studied for a variety of applications in various fields.3 The very first example of an endohedral fullerene is actually an EMF involving encapsulation of the lanthanum ion in C60.1 Encapsulation of metal atom(s) imparts certain unique properties to the fullerenes which are non-existent in pristine fullerene.3 Endohedral fullerene complexes4 have found potential applications in diverse areas of science and technology including molecular medicine,5–9 renewable energy10,11 and optoelectronics.12 In the field of medicine, EMFs have found applications as MRI contrast agents6,13–19 X-ray contrast agents,20 radiotracers21,22 radiopharmaceuticals, and as anti-tumor22–24 and anti-microbial agents.25 EMFs are used in organic photovoltaics as new acceptors for polymeric solar cells (PSCs)26,27 and donor acceptor dyads.28,29 EMFs have also found applications in photo-electrochemistry cells,30 superconductors, metallofullerene lasers,31–33 ferroelectric materials,34,35 memory devices32 and quantum computers.36–40 Metal encapsulation also imparts unique reactivity to fullerenes for exohedral chemical reactions. A few important reactions in this regard are retro-cycloaddition,41–43 radical addition reaction44 and unique reactivity in several other reactions.45–47

Over the past two decades, a number of hybrid or non-carbon fullerene-like structures have also appeared in the literature with varying numbers of atoms. Gold and boron nano-cages are prominent examples of homo-nuclear non-carbon nano-cages. Gold nano-cages have widespread applications in quite diverse areas of chemistry.48 Group IV carbides and group III phosphides and nitrides are important members of hybrid nano-cages. The hybrid nano-cages have quite remarkable chemical and physical properties. These nanostructures are large band gap semiconductor materials.49–53

Fullerene-like group III nitrides and phosphides can exist in a variety of sizes. A number of theoretical studies had been focused on revealing the relative stabilities of different sized nano-cages. Fowler et al. theoretically demonstrated that B12N12, B16N16 and B28N28 are stable BN fullerenes, however, the B12N12 cluster is the most stable among all studied nano-cages.54 Wu et al.50 studied the relative stabilities of AlnNn nano-cages (n = 2–41) through disproportionation energy and binding energy per AlN unit. The Al12N12 cluster with Th symmetry is the most stable nano-cage, and it can be considered as an ideal inorganic fullerene-like candidate. Beheshtian et al.55 studied the stability, geometry and electronic structure of Al12N12, Al12P12, B12N12, and B12P12 using DFT calculations. These nano-cages have also been synthesized experimentally and detected using time of flight mass spectrometry.56,57 The nano-cages are spherical in shape and consist of four- and six-membered rings.

The X12Y12 nano-cages are spherical in shape with almost zero dipole moment and zero hyperpolarizability. Moreover, these nano-cages have large HOMO–LUMO gaps. However, their electronic and optical properties can be modulated by doping with metal atoms. Xuri Huang and co-workers58 have shown that the electronic and non-linear optical properties of the Al12N12 nano-cage are significantly enhanced by doping with alkali metal atoms. Different positions are considered for decoration of alkali metals on the surface of the nano-cage. The band gaps for alkali metal doped AlN nano-cages are 0.49–0.71 eV. Moreover, the first hyperpolarizability as high as 8.8 × 105 was calculated for Li@b64-Al12N12, where b64 indicates that the lithium atom is oriented on a bond shared between four- and six-membered rings of the nano-cage.58 The dramatic increase in the hyperpolarizability is attributed to excess diffuse electrons generated by doping of the nano-cage with alkali metal atoms. A number of theoretical studies59–67 revealed that systems containing diffuse electrons such as electrides and alkalides possess large first hyperpolarizabilities. For example, the static first hyperpolarizability of the (H2O)3{e} anion is 1.72 × 107 a.u., which is dramatically larger than the value of 35 a.u. for (H2O)3.68 Similarly, (FH)2{e}(HF) has a very high static first hyperpolarizability of 9.2 × 106 a.u.59

The remarkable study of Xuri Huang and coworkers is inspiring to further explore the electronic and non-linear optical (NLO) properties of alkali metal doped nano-cages. In the present study, the electronic and non-linear optical properties of alkali metal encapsulated X12Y12 nano-cages (X = Al, B and Y = N, P) have been explored. In this regard, phosphide nano-cages are of great interest because the effect of metal doping on the NLO properties of any phosphide nano-cage is not reported in the literature. The present study is detailed enough for a comparative analysis of the effect of alkali metal doping on different X12Y12 nano-cages. Another objective of this study is to find the barriers for alkali metals to cross the boundary of the nano-cages. The formation of EMFs had been a challenge since their first discovery. Various approaches had been applied to produce endohedral fullerene complexes with different guest species. The most common approach is laser vaporization of graphite sheets intercalated with metal salts or high energy biomolecular collision between host fullerene and guest species.1,69 The molecular surgery method has also been applied to encapsulate guest molecules in fullerenes.70–73 Transition metal encapsulated X12Y12 type nano-cages have been synthesized but through the arc melting process and characterized by high resolution electron microscopy (HREM).57,74 It will be really interesting if an alkali atom is encapsulated in a nano-cage by translating through the boundary of the nano-cage. A study of such type is reported for all-carbon C60 fullerene where a hydrogen atom is shown to pass through the center of the pentagon and hexagon and rattle inside the cage.75 However, the literature is completely silent regarding the motion of alkali metals through either C60 or X12Y12 nano-cages. Movement of alkali metals into the nano-cages with low activation barriers combined with a high stability of EMFs would provide an easy path for the synthesis of EMFs. Moreover, higher barriers for exclusion of alkali metals through the nano-cages are highly desirable. The present study illustrates the results from dynamics, electronics and non-linear optical properties of alkali metal encapsulated X12Y12 nano-cages.

Computational methods

All calculations were performed by using the GAUSSIAN 09 program package.76 The geometries of all structures were optimized without any symmetry constraints at the B3LYP method of density functional theory using the 6-31G(d,p) basis set. The B3LYP method of DFT is well known to model the geometries of inorganic nano-cages.55,77,78 All doped structures (alkali metal encapsulated nano-cages) were optimized in the doublet spin state. The 〈S2〉 values of the optimized geometries are also evaluated to find out any spin contamination. The 〈S2〉 values for all EMFs range from 0.75 to 0.76 which is very close to the value of 0.75 for a pure doublet state. The 〈S2〉 values clearly suggest that these systems do not suffer from spin contamination. Frequency analysis (with harmonic approximation) was performed at the same level to confirm that these optimized geometries correspond to the true minima (the absence of any imaginary frequencies). The vibrational spectrum is extracted from frequency analysis.

The stabilities of these alkali metal encapsulated nano-cages are judged from binding energies which are calculated at the ωB97XD method of DFT. ωB97XD is a long-range and dispersion corrected method79 which is well known to predict non-covalent interaction energies.80 This method has also been shown recently81 to predict the interaction energies of alkali metal complexes to an accuracy of 0.1 kcal mol−1. Moreover, this method is also well known for studying potential energy surfaces.81 The interaction energy is defined as:

 
Eb = E(M@X12Y12) − (E(X12Y12) + E(M))(1)
where E(X12Y12), E(M), and E(M@X12Y12) are the total energies of the undoped X12Y12 molecule, the alkali atom M, and the corresponding doped system M@X12Y12, respectively. Distortion (expansion) of the nano-cage caused by alkali metal encapsulation was estimated through distortion energy. For the estimation of distortion energy (Edist), the geometry of the metal encapsulated nano-cage is taken, the metal atom is removed and the single point energy of the expanded nano-cage is calculated (EX12Y12(expanded)). Distortion energy is defined as:
 
Edist = EX12Y12(expanded) − EX12Y12(2)
The barrier associated with boundary crossing of the nano-cage by the alkali metal (through the hexagon of the nano-cage) is obtained by scanning the potential energy surface. For this purpose, two face-to-face hexagons of the nano-cage are selected (A and B, see Fig. 1). The distance between hexagon A and the alkali metal is scanned from 9 Å to 1 Å in such a way that the alkali metal atom passes through hexagon B during this movement as shown in Fig. 1.

Results and discussion

The geometries of alkali metal doped structures

The optimized geometries of pure Al12N12 (AlN), Al12P12 (AlP), B12N12 (BN) and B12P12 (BP) nano-cages reveal that each nano-cage is composed of four- and six-membered rings. The optimized geometric parameters for the nano-cages are well documented in the literature; therefore, a detailed description is not provided here. The discussion is restricted to those geometric parameters which have not been well discussed previously. The geometric parameters obtained in this study agree well with those in the literature.55

The numbering scheme for the discussion of the geometries of the nano-cages is shown in Fig. 2, whereas the optimized geometries of the alkali metal encapsulated nano-cages (AlN, AlP, BN and BP) are shown in Fig. 3. A quick glance at the optimized geometries reveals that lithium is not encapsulated at the center of AlP and BN nano-cages (displaced towards one side); however, a close look (Tables 1 and 2) reveals that alkali metals are not encapsulated exactly at the center of the nano-cage except for in the Na@AlN, K@AlN and K@BP nano-cages. Displacement of lithium from the center of the nano-cage towards one side is more pronounced than that of sodium or potassium. Probably, the smaller size of lithium does not permit coordination from all atoms of the nano-cage; therefore, displacement towards one side is observed. Moreover, the displacement is more pronounced in AlP and BN nano-cages. The different behavior of lithium encapsulation (compared to potassium and sodium) can be attributed to the small size of lithium. The different geometries of lithium encapsulated nano-cages impart certain unique electronic and optical properties (vide infra) compared to sodium and potassium encapsulation.

The lithium atom is slightly displaced from the center of the AlN nano-cage which results in slightly different Li–Al and Li–N bond lengths. The Li–Al bond lengths range from 2.82 to 2.85 Å. A similar range of bond lengths is observed for Li–N bonds. The Li–N bond lengths range from 2.96 to 2.98 Å. As mentioned above, Na@AlN and K@AlN complexes contain the alkali metal exactly at the center of the nano-cage; therefore, all M–Al bonds are of the same length (similar to the case for M–N bond lengths); Na–Al and Na–N bond lengths in Na@AlN are 2.86 and 2.96 Å, respectively. Similarly, K–Al and K–N bond lengths in K@AlN are 2.90 and 2.96 Å, respectively. The encapsulation of alkali metal atoms in nano-cages also causes expansion of the nano-cages (vide infra).

The lithium atom is displaced much away from the center of the AlP nano-cage. The lithium atom is shifted more close to a six-membered ring of the nano-cage (hexagon) which results in certain very short and very long Li–Al and Li–P bonds. The Li–Al7, Li–Al9 and Li–Al11 bond lengths are 2.70 Å each. Similarly, the Li–P14, Li–P20 and Li–P23 bond lengths are 3.04 Å. The weak Li–Al and Li–P bond lengths are 3.97 Å (Li–Al1, Li–Al3 and Li–Al6) and 4.68 Å (Li–P16, Li–P19 and Li–P24), respectively. Other Li–Al bond distances range from 3.10 to 3.56 Å. Small fluctuations in Na–Al and Na–P bonds are observed in the Na@AlP nano-cage. The Na–Al bond lengths range from 3.23 to 3.27 Å, whereas the Na–P bond lengths range from 3.83 to 3.91 Å. The effect of potassium encapsulation on the geometric parameters of the AlP nano-cage is similar to that of sodium encapsulation. The K–Al bond lengths range from 3.37 to 3.39 Å, whereas the K–P bond lengths range from 3.86–3.89 Å.

The effect of encapsulated alkali metals on the geometric properties of the BN nano-cage is very similar to that of the encapsulation of alkali metals in the AlP nano-cage. The lithium atom is displaced away from the center of the nano-cage towards a hexagon. The Li–N1, Li–N2 and Li–N3 bond lengths are 2.14, 2.11 and 2.14 Å. Similarly, the Li–B21, Li–B22 and Li–B23 bond lengths are 2.12, 2.13 and 2.05 Å, respectively. Other Li–N and Li–B bond lengths range between 2.34–2.69 and 2.12–2.43 Å, respectively. The Na–N bond lengths in Na@BN range from 2.33 to 2.48 Å, whereas the Na–B bond lengths range from 2.22–2.49 Å. The K–N bond lengths in K@BN range from 2.40–2.47 Å, whereas the K–B bond lengths range from 2.29–2.53 Å.

Alkali metal encapsulated BP nano-cages show a relatively narrow range of bond lengths (compared to other nano-cages). For example, the Li–B bond lengths range from 2.70–2.76 Å, whereas the Li–P bond lengths are 3.18–3.23 Å. Similarly, the Na–B bond lengths in the Na@BP nano-cage are calculated to range between 2.74 to 2.78 Å, whereas the Na–P bond lengths range over 3.19–3.23 Å. The K@BP nano-cage is highly symmetrical where the bond lengths are 2.81 and 3.22 Å for K–B and K–N bonds, respectively.

Energetic analysis

Adsorption energies. The stability of an endohedral complex is an important criterion for the realization of the system. In this regard, a number of different energetic parameters are calculated with the intention to correlate the geometric parameters with energetic ones. Binding energies of alkali metal atoms with nano-cages for all EMF complexes are given in Table 3. The encapsulation of potassium metal in nano-cages is an endothermic process, whereas the encapsulation of sodium is endothermic for nitride nano-cages. On the other hand, the encapsulation of lithium in all nano-cages is an exothermic process except for the BN nano-cage where a large positive value for binding energy is calculated (48.99 kcal mol−1). Some general trends can be traced regarding the binding energies of alkali metals in nano-cages. For any nano-cage, the endothermicity of the encapsulation increases with the size of the alkali metal (lowest for lithium and highest for potassium). For example, the binding energies for encapsulation of lithium, sodium and potassium in the AlN nano-cage are −2.78, 22.56 and 71.72 kcal mol−1, respectively. Similarly, the corresponding binding energies in the AlP nano-cage are −26.06, −12.88 and 17.54 kcal mol−1, respectively. The encapsulation of alkali metals in the BN nano-cage is highly endothermic. The binding energies are 48.99, 115.66 and 267.5 kcal mol−1 for the encapsulation of lithium, sodium and potassium (in the BN nano-cage), respectively.

Moreover, the encapsulation of alkali metals in nitride nano-cages (AlN and BN) is more endothermic than the corresponding phosphide nano-cages (AlP and BP, see Table 3). This observation can be explained on steric grounds because phosphide nano-cages have a large diameter compared to nitride nano-cages (vide infra), and can easily encapsulate alkali metal atoms. This statement is also supported by the fact that the endothermicity of the encapsulation increases with an increase in the size of the alkali metal.

It was quite interesting to see whether encapsulation causes expansion of the nano-cage, and what is the energetic cost for this expansion. The expansion of the nano-cage is calculated by the diameter of the nano-cages between two face-to-face hexagons (A and B, Fig. 1). Analysis of the data reveals that the encapsulation of alkali metal atoms causes expansion of the nano-cages except for lithium encapsulation in the AlP nano-cage where the diameter of the nano-cage decreases to 5.38 from 5.45 Å in the bare AlP nano-cage. The decrease in diameter is probably due to favorable coordination of phosphate with the alkali metal atoms. The diameters of bare AlN, AlP, BN and BP nano-cages are 4.44, 5.45, 3.55 and 4.55 Å, respectively.

The diameters of metal encapsulated nano-cages increase regularly with the increase in the size of the metal. For example, the diameters of lithium, sodium and potassium doped AlN nano-cages are 4.48, 4.50 and 4.52 Å, respectively. Similarly, the diameters of lithium, sodium and potassium encapsulated BN nano-cages are 3.62, 3.65 and 3.67 Å respectively, compared to the value of 3.55 Å for the bare BN nano-cage. The expansion of the nano-cage caused by encapsulation is relatively less pronounced for phosphide nano-cages. The diameter of sodium and potassium encapsulated AlP nano-cages are 5.47 and 5.56 Å, respectively, compared to the value of 5.45 Å for the bare AlP nano-cage. Similarly, the diameters of lithium, sodium and potassium encapsulated BP nano-cages are 4.58, 4.63 and 4.64 Å, respectively, compared to the value of 4.55 Å for the bare BP nano-cage. The less pronounced expansion of phosphide nano-cages can be attributed to the large size of the nano-cages where steric effects are negligible.

Distortion energies. The encapsulation of alkali metal atoms causes expansion of the nano-cage, and it can expected that this expansion might contribute negatively towards binding energies. Therefore, the energetic cost for expansion of the encapsulated nano-cage was calculated next (Table 3). For this purpose, the optimized geometry of the metal encapsulated nano-cage was taken, the alkali metal was removed and the single point energy was computed for the expanded nano-cage. The difference in energy between this expanded nano-cage and the true minima is taken as the measure of expansion energy (distortion energy, Edist). The Edist for the nano-cage increases with the size of the alkali metal. The Edist values for lithium, sodium and potassium encapsulation in AlN nano-cages are 6.22, 14.4 and 30.5 kcal mol−1, whereas the overall energies of encapsulation are −2.78, 22.56 and 71.72 kcal mol−1, respectively. The comparison reveals that the distortion energy is a significant contributor to the endothermicity of encapsulation. The expansion energies of Li, Na and K doped AlP nano-cages are 10.1, 0.12 and 12.1 kcal mol−1, respectively. The situation is considerably changed for boron nano-cages. The expansion energies for alkali metal encapsulated BN nano-cages are much smaller than the Ebind which suggests that some other factors (such as steric strain) dominate for BN nano-cages. The expansion energies of Li, Na and K encapsulated BN nano-cages are 48.4, 56.3 and 85.2 kcal mol−1 compared to 48.99, 115.66 and 267.5 kcal mol−1 binding energies, respectively. The lowest thermodynamic costs for ring expansion are observed for BP nano-cages where the Edist values are 0.6, 17.9 and 17.5 kcal mol−1, respectively, for Li, Na and K encapsulated BP nano-cages.

Attention was next paid to two very fundamental but interesting questions; can alkali metals cross the boundary of nano-cage? and what is the kinetic barrier for such a process? The literature is completely silent in this regard. The only report describes the motion of the hydrogen atom through the pentagon and hexagon of pristine C60 fullerene. The potential energy surface is scanned for the movement of an alkali atom into the nano-cage (see the Computational methodology for details), and the results are given in Table 3. The six-membered ring of the nano-cage can provide a better pathway for the encapsulation and exclusion of the alkali metal, therefore, the movement of the alkali metal is scanned through the six-membered ring of the nano-cage. The barrier to cross the boundary of nano-cages is highly dependent on the size and nature of the nano-cages. The crossing barriers are much higher for nitrogen containing nano-cages compared to phosphorus nano-cages which can be attributed to the smaller size of the former. The highest barriers for boundary crossing are observed for the boron nitride nano-cage. The barriers for crossing are 216, 697 and 1315 kcal mol−1 for lithium, sodium and potassium atom through the BN nano-cage, respectively. The high barrier for movement of potassium through the BN nano-cage can be attributed to the small ring size of the BN nano-cage and the large atomic radius of potassium (compared to lithium and sodium) (Fig. 4).

Electronic properties

Frontier molecular orbitals determine the electronic properties of a system. For example, the HOMO describes the ability of a system to lose an electron, whereas the LUMO illustrates its ability to accept an electron. Therefore, the negative energies of the HOMO and LUMO can be taken approximately equal to the ionization potential and electron affinity, respectively (according to the Koopmans theorem).96 The band gap analysis reveals that group III–V nano-cages have properties between a large band gap semiconductor and an insulator. The band gaps for pure AlN, AlP and BP nano-cages are 3.93, 3.39 and 3.70 eV, respectively, which indicate their large band gap semiconductor nature.

On the other hand, the band gap for the BN nano-cage is 6.85 eV,97–101 which illustrates its insulator-like properties. The large band gap of the nano-cages is prohibitive to their potential applications in nano-scale devices. The encapsulation of alkali metals in these nano-cages brings about remarkable changes in the electronic structures, and band gaps are considerably reduced. The band gaps for alkali metal encapsulated AlN, AlP and BP nano-cages are between 0.4 to 2.0 eV, whereas the band gaps for the corresponding BN nano-cages are between 2.05 and 2.64 eV. The band gaps for alkali metal encapsulated nano-cages are about 11 to 55% of the original values for pure nano-cages. In general, the encapsulated metal atom increases the energies of the HOMOs and LUMOs (particularly for the aluminum nano-cages where the effect is almost comparable); however, the situation is somewhat different for the BN nano-cages where the HOMO is increased in energy but the LUMO is decreased in energy. The opposing effect on the energies of HOMOs and LUMOs dramatically decreases the band gap (EHL). For BN nano cages, the energies of HOMOs and LUMOs are −7.71 and −0.86 eV; however, the energies of HOMOs (LUMOs) for Li@BN, Na@BN and K@BN are −4.02 (−1.38), −3.84 (−1.59) and −3.97 (−1.92) eV, respectively. The band gaps are reduced from 6.85 eV for the bare BN nano-cage to 2.64 eV, 2.25 eV and 2.05 eV for Li@BN, Na@BN and K@BN, respectively.

For BN, AlN and AlP nano-cages, the effect of encapsulated alkali metals on the energies of HOMOs and LUMOs is in the order K > Na > Li however, the effect on the energies of LUMOs of BP nano-cages follows the order; Li > Na > K. For example, the energies of the HOMO and LUMO of Li@BP are −4.19 and −2.69 eV, respectively, compared to −6.83 eV and −3.13 eV for the HOMO and LUMO of the bare BP nano-cage. The effect of encapsulated sodium and potassium atoms is much higher. The energies of HOMOs (LUMOs) are −4.14 (−2.77) and −3.66 (−3.26) eV for Na@BP and K@BP nano-cage, respectively (Fig. 6).

Moreover, the effect of encapsulated alkali metals on the energies of HOMOs and LUMOs is different for AlP nano-cages, compared to other nano-cages. Encapsulated alkali metal atoms increase the energies of HOMOs and LUMOs in the order K > Na > Li for AlP nano-cages.

The energies of HOMOs (LUMOs) are −4.73 (−2.97), −4.58 (−2.8) and −4.23 (−2.49) eV for Li@AlP, Na@AlP and K@AlP, respectively. Comparable changes in the energies of HOMOs and LUMOs on encapsulation result in very comparable band gaps for metal encapsulated nano-cages. The resulting band gaps are 1.76, 1.78 and 1.74 eV for Li@AlP, Na@AlP and K@AlP, respectively.

A similar situation is observed for the AlN nano-cage where comparable effects are observed on the energies of HOMOs and LUMOs of M@AlN. The band gaps are 1.86, 1.85 and 1.75 eV for Li@AlN, Na@AlN and K@AlN, respectively. The effect of metal encapsulation on the band gap is more pronounced for boron nano-cages compared to aluminum nano-cages. For boron nano-cages the band gaps are reduced to about 11–41% of the original values, whereas this value for aluminum nano-cages is 45–53%. The lowest band gap of 0.4 eV is observed for the K@BP nano-cage. The band gap for the K@BP nano-cage reflects its metal-like characteristics.

Encapsulation of alkali metal atoms decreases the band gap for all nano-cages irrespective of the nature of the nano-cage and alkali metal; however the quantitative effect is dependent on the nature of the metal and nano-cage. Under the action of lone pairs of nitrogen and phosphorus in nano-cages the outer s valence electrons of alkali metals are expected to push out to become diffuse excess electrons which can significantly increase the energy of the newly formed HOMO.

This newly formed HOMO is expected to reside between the original HOMO and LUMO of the nano-cages. Under the action of diffuse excess electrons the original HOMO becomes HOMO−1 in encapsulated nano-cages. These encapsulated nano-cages are n-type semiconductors. Under the influence of lone pairs of nitrogen and phosphorus atoms of the nano-cages, outer electrons of alkali metals are pushed out, and one should expect that the HOMO should reside on alkali metals or should have a contribution from alkali metals. Although the encapsulation of alkali metals increases the energies of HOMOs for all systems, the participation of alkali metals in HOMOs of different systems is quite different. The HOMOs of Li@AlP, Na@AlP and Li@BP show clear participation from alkali metals, whereas other encapsulated fullerenes do not show any such participation from alkali metals. In this regard, Li@AlP and Na@AlP are of particular interest because the HOMOs in these systems are mainly located on alkali metals, with a small contribution from the nano-cage part. The participation of alkali metals in HOMOs of these complexes is expected to significantly enhance the electronic and nonlinear optical properties of these encapsulated nano-cages, and indeed this is the case (vide infra). PDOS analysis provides further insight into the contribution of different fragments in frontier molecular orbitals. The PDOS analysis reveals that the situation is quite different for different EMFs. The behaviour of aluminum nano-cages is different from that of boron nano-cages. The HOMOs in aluminum nano-cages have densities on alkali metal atoms besides the nano-cage moiety, whereas the HOMOs in boron nano-cages have no such contribution from alkali metals.

Moreover, the participation of alkali metals in HOMOs decreases with the increase in the size of the alkali metals. For example, densities of HOMOs in lithium and sodium in Li@AlN and Na@AlN, respectively, (Fig. 9) are greater than the nano-cage moiety; however, the density on K in K@AlN is lower than the nano-cage part. A similar trend is also seen for AlP nano-cages but to a larger extent where the contribution of potassium in the HOMO becomes negligible.

The HOMOs in boron nano-cages (Fig. 10) have almost zero contribution from alkali metals except for the Li@BP nano-cage where lithium has some contribution to the HOMO. These results suggest that coordination of nitrogen or phosphorus in these nano-cages does not cause the outer s electrons of alkali metals to become diffuse excess electrons. The interaction between alkali metals and nano-cages is probably not effective enough to cause significant changes in the electronic and optical properties of these EMFs. The weak interaction of boron nano-cages with alkali metal atoms in EMFs can be rationalized on charge density analysis. The charges on nitrogen and phosphorus atoms in aluminum nano-cages are more pronounced than boron nano-cages, probably due to the greater electropositivity of the former. The higher electronic density on nitrogen and phosphorus in aluminum nano-cages can be easily transferred to alkali metal atoms. The stronger interaction of nitrogen and phosphorus thus generates the diffuse excess electrons on alkali metals in these nano-cages. On the other hand, the negative charge on nitrogen and phosphorus in boron nano-cages is not pronounced enough to generate diffuse excess electrons on alkali metals in EMFs. Obviously, doping alkali metal atoms can be a simple and effective approach to decrease the large EHL gap of the inorganic nano-cages. These EMFs show an n-type semiconducting behavior, which can be helpful in the application of these nano-cages in electronic devices.

A two-level model gives significant information to explain the remarkable enhancement of hyperpolarizability; therefore, the two-level model is taken into account when explaining the exceptionally high hyperpolarizabilities of Li@BP and K@BP nano-cages. According to the two-level model

β0 ∝ Δμ·f/ΔE03
where ΔE, f0, and Δμ are the crucial transition energy, the largest oscillator strength, and the difference in dipole moment between the ground state and the crucial excited state (the excited state with the largest oscillator strength), respectively. TD-DFT calculations have been performed to calculate the characteristics of the crucial excited states. All encapsulated nano-cages share a common feature that the crucial excited states involve a transition from the HOMO to some higher orbitals (see ESI†). The lone pairs of nitrogen or phosphorus in nano-cages cause the valence s electrons of alkali metals to become excess diffuse electrons, which causes the HOMO to be mainly composed of s orbitals of alkali metals (aluminum nano-cages). These diffuse excess electrons are held very loosely with the core of the alkali metals, and are therefore, easy to excite. This results in a decrease in excitation energies. The excitation energies of alkali metal encapsulated nano-cages decrease remarkably which causes a significant increase in hyperpolarizability because the latter (hyperpolarizability) depends inversely on the third power of excitation energy. The decrease in excitation energy is observed for all systems whether diffuse excess electrons are generated or not. The exceptionally high hyperpolarizability values for Li@BP and K@BP nano-cages are attributed to very small energy required for crucial excitation.

Monotonic vs. non-monotonic behavior

The trends of first hyperpolarizabilities of alkali metal encapsulated nano-cages are perplexing, at least to some extent. For most of the nano-cages, the hyperpolarizabilities are higher for lithium or potassium encapsulated systems, and the lowest for sodium. If the presence of diffuse excess electrons is the sole factor then one would expect a monotonic increase in hyperpolarizability with an increase in the atomic number of the alkali metal because the presence of diffuse excess electrons is related to the ionization potential. The ionization potential increases with an increase in the atomic number of an alkali metal. This type of monotonic increase in hyperpolarizability with an increase in the atomic number is reported in the literature (although not prevailing in this case) for M@Calix[4]pyrrole (M = Li, Na, and K) and M2˙ + TCNQ˙ − (M = Li, Na, and K).62,65,102

Another factor which can also significantly contribute to hyperpolarizability is the interaction distance between the alkali metal and the system of interest. Short distances illustrate a better interaction between the alkali metal and the nano-cage, and can lead to significantly high hyperpolarizabilities. Generally, the interaction distances are in the order lithium < sodium < potassium. If the interaction distance is the sole factor for deciding the hyperpolarizabilities then the monotonic decrease in hyperpolarizability can be expected with an increase in the atomic number. This type of behavior is also well documented in the literature for M@pyrrole (M = Li and Na) systems[25] with the alkali Li/Na atom located over the pyrrole ring, and also for alkali metal decoration on the AlN nano-cage.

The situation is more complex for metal encapsulated X12Y12 nano-cages because ionization potentials as well as distances between the alkali metal and coordinating nitrogen or phosphorus atoms play a crucial role in deciding the hyperpolarizabilities. Lithium encapsulated nano-cages are generally polarized in bonding where the lithium atom shifts towards one side and interacts with certain atoms of the nano-cages more strongly which is probably responsible for the higher hyperpolarizabilities of lithium encapsulated nano-cages. Phosphide nano-cages which show more pronounced polarization in bonding with the lithium atom show even higher hyperpolarizabilities. Encapsulated sodium and potassium atoms cause expansion of the nano-cage (more pronounced for potassium) which significantly increases the distance between the nano-cage and the alkali metal, and decreases the hyperpolarizability. A slight increase in hyperpolarizability for potassium encapsulated nano-cages over sodium encapsulated nano-cages is probably attributed to the small ionization potential of the former.

For the two-level expression, TD-DFT calculations were initially performed on M@BP (M = Li, Na and K) systems, and the computed results revealed that potassium atoms bring about a significant drop in the energy for the crucial transition which is responsible for high hyperpolarizability. The next low transition energy is calculated for Li@BP and the highest transition energy is observed for the sodium encapsulated nano-cage (Na@BP). This trend of transition energies perfectly matches with the trend of hyperpolarizabilities.

Hyperpolarizability calculated through the two-level expression agrees with the calculated hyperpolarizability for aluminum nano-cages (compared to boron nano-cages). For example, the hyperpolarizabilities for Li@AlN, Na@AlN and K@AlN according to the two-level expression are 156.8, 75.9 and 149.8 a.u., respectively, and this order is consistent with hyperpolarizabilities of 453.3, 35.6, 49.3 a.u. for Li@AlN, Na@AlN and K@AlN, respectively. This type of agreement is not observed for boron nano-cages, and this can be attributed to the fact that the coordination of nitrogen and phosphorus in boron nano-cages does not lead to the generation of diffuse excess electrons.

Infra-red analysis

AlN, AlP, BN and BP nano-cages are studied to evaluate the changes in their infra-red spectral characteristics upon the encapsulation of alkali metals. The results are presented in Fig. 12 and Tables S2–S5 (ESI†). In general, the encapsulation of alkali metals in nano-cages decreases the vibrational frequency. The decrease in stretching vibration may be attributed to the expansion of the nano-cage upon encapsulation which weakens the bond between aluminum and nitrogen. The expansion of the nano-cage is more pronounced for larger alkali metal atoms which would further decrease the Al–N bond strength, and this is consistent with the calculated vibrational spectrum. Besides shifts in the vibrational frequencies of the nano-cages, some new peaks are observed.

The number of stretching vibrations is increased for BN nano-cages upon encapsulation. For example, the B–N stretching vibration at 1424 cm−1 is replaced by a number of stretching vibrations at 1440, 1413, 1404, 1384 and 1347 cm−1 in Li@BN. A similar behavior is also observed for Na@BN and K@BN nano-cages. This behavior can be attributed to the unsymmetrical encapsulation of alkali metals in BN nano-cages. This notion is further supported by the fact that similar splitting of a vibration is also observed for the Li@AlP nano-cage in which lithium is encapsulated unsymmetrically.

Conclusions

Density functional theory calculations have been performed for alkali metal encapsulated X12Y12 nano-cages (X = B, Al and Y = N, P) to evaluate their stability (kinetic and thermodynamic) and optical (linear and non-linear) properties. The geometric analysis reveals that the encapsulated lithium atom is much displaced from the center of the nano-cage, whereas sodium and potassium atoms reside at or close to the center of the nano-cage. The encapsulation of potassium metal in nano-cages is an endothermic process, whereas the encapsulation of lithium in all nano-cages is an exothermic process except for the BN nano-cage where a large positive binding energy is calculated (52.08 kcal mol−1). The encapsulation of sodium is endothermic and exothermic in nitride and phosphide nano-cages, respectively. Distortion (expansion) of the nano-cage caused by alkali metal encapsulation was estimated through the distortion energy, which shows a good correlation with the diameter of the nano-cage. Distortion energies range from 0.1 kcal mol−1 for Na@AlP to 85.2 kcal mol−1 for the K@BN nano-cage. The distortion energies contribute significantly to the endothermic encapsulation of larger alkali metal atoms. Kinetic barriers for the movement of alkali metals through nano-cages (boundary crossing barriers, EBC) are quantitatively measured. The lowest crossing barrier is calculated for the AlP nano-cage where the lithium atom can cross the boundary with a barrier as low as 31 kcal mol−1, a barrier which can be easily surpassed at a temperature just above room temperature. This manuscript presents the first ever study on boundary crossing barriers for alkali metal atoms through any spherical surface. The translation of alkali metals through the boundary of nano-cages presents a new approach for the encapsulation of metals in nano-cages. The encapsulation of alkali metal atoms in nano-cages dramatically reduces the band gap. The band gap for K@BP is only 0.4 eV which suggests that the conductivity of this EMF should be comparable to that of metals. The reduced band gaps lead to prominent changes in linear and non-liner optical properties. The linear and non-linear optical properties of alkali metal encapsulated nano-cages were calculated. A quite remarkable non-linear optical response is calculated for lithium and potassium encapsulated BP nano-cages. In general, the non-linear optical response of phosphide nano-cages is two to three orders of magnitude higher than that of nitride nano-cages. The calculated first hyperpolarizability of the K@BP nano-cage is 5.7 × 105 a.u., a value comparable to that of the best NLO material reported in the literature. The electronic structures of nano-cages including the HOMO–LUMO gap, TDOS, PDOS and excitation energies are analyzed to rationalize the extraordinary NLO response of phosphide nano-cages. The NLO response of the K@BP nano-cage is primarily attributed to a very low excitation energy (0.5 eV). The interaction of nitrogen and phosphorus (of nano-cages) with alkali metals differs between aluminum and boron nano-cages. The interaction of lone pair containing atoms with alkali metals in aluminum nano-cages generates diffuse excess electrons, whereas no such excess diffuse electrons are generated in boron nano-cages. The UV-Vis and infra-red spectral characteristics for these encapsulated nano-cages are evaluated as a guide for future studies.
